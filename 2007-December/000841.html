Hi Pieter,

In comparison to US commercial stations, Q-Music is a
wealth of diversity :-)). To give you an example, we
have a local 'rock' station here, and they only play
Marilyn Manson, Nine Inch Nails, Nickelback and those
kinds of things. Nothing older than 3 months. They
have a playlist of about 50 songs. About 1/3 the time
is devoted to commercials ('Get a Dodge 1500 Pickup
with 60 month financing and 0% APR for approved
customers (=nobody)'), and 'station identification
calls' (this is radio XYZ Atlanta, the best station in
the world, etc etc). I live in Atlanta GA, and radio
here is completely segregated racially: there are
'black' stations which only play hip-hop, there are
stations which only play soul, and then there are
'white' stations which only play 'rock' or 'soft pop'.

The absolute ultimate in formatting is satellite
radio, like Sirius and XM. They have 160 channels,
each of which with their only category of music. For
example, XM has 3 (!) channels with different flavours
of 'punk'. 5 channels of Elvis Presley (hits, movie
soundtracks, slow songs, you name it), and for
instance some 'oldies' channels that only play Bob
Dylan and Simon & Garfunkel. In other words, when you
tune in to a channel, you here the exact same kind of
music over and over again, which to me is offensive. I
like to hear a rock song, with maybe some hiphop after
that, followed by a 60s soul classic, then a disco
hit, followed by Bruce Springsteen. But there are NO
stations in the US that will play this on the air.

I usually download podcasts from KX-radio, a
format-free internet station from the Netherlands.
They broadcast like any 'standard' radio station, but
every hour is available as a podcast as well. I
donwload their programs, and burn them on CDs to play
in my car.

Also interesting for a European is how fast radio
stations change here. Radio stations are bought and
sold on a weekly basis, and the station that
broadcasts type X music may restart as a completely
different station next week (hey, new ownership!). In
the Netherlands, a commercial radio station can only
get a license if they demonstrate beforehand that they
will contribute something new, and that their business
model is valid. Without that, no license. The result
is that most stations are somewhat well thought out,
and they usually survive (including the 'musical
wallpaper stations' like Q-music, Caz!, Radio 10, Sky
Radio, etc).

Anyway. The biggest drawback of everybody making their
own podcast still is that the content is scattered,
and of highly varying quality. Some podcasts are OK,
others are a waste of time, but it takes time to find
out, unfortunately.  

Anyway.

Wilfred
